Privatization should be promoted.

Privatization refers either to selling public property (e.g., a park) or to outsourcing services (e.g., schools, prisons, parking meters) to private companies.

Reasons Why

  • Privatization is good for public budgets.

    Many towns and states have trouble making ends meet. Broken budgets lead to higher taxes and sometimes bankruptcy. Privatization means cash up front, and fewer expenses down the road.

  • Private companies are not driven by politics.

    Private companies care about the bottom line, so they focus on getting things done rather than on political favors, rivalries, and apathy.

  • Privatization is the American way.

    Where possible, we should harness the power of private enterprise to solve our problems. Government-run enterprise should be a last resort.

  • Privatization is good for the consumer.

    Compare your last DMV experience with visiting the AAA. Private companies work better because they're accountable to their shareholders, and because they specialize in doing one thing well.

Reasons Why Not

  • Privatization means handing over control of public assets to private interests.

    When we privatize a public asset, we lose our say in how it's run. Costs can go up and service can go down and there's nothing we can do about it.

  • Communities that privatize public assets end up wishing they hadn't.

    Sadly, by the time they discover what privatization does, these communities are locked into contracts drafted by highly skilled lawyers.

  • Privatization contracts are much too long.

    Contracts that last as long as 20 or even 50 years are the norm, so that the private company can avoid competition for as long as possible.

  • Monopolies should never be held by private companies.

    Water systems or parking meters are monopolies. Monopolies invite price-gouging (profiteering). The best way to avoid this risk is to keep monopolies public.

  • Profit-driven doesn't mean we get the profits.

    Businesses can be more efficient than government. But the increased efficiency ends up in the pockets of CEOs and shareholders rather than in the taxpayer's pockets.

  • Cost-cutting isn't necessarily a good thing.

    Advocates of privatization like to talk about the savings it will bring. But savings usually come at the cost of cutting corners — like hiring a poorly paid, low-skilled workforce.
